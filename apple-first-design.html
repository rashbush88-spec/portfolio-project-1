Why Apple-first design matters for SMBs

Abdul-Rashid Bushran · MarshalTech Hub · 5 min read

Running MarshalTech Hub — where I sell iPhones and MacBooks alongside building software — puts me in an unusual position. I see firsthand what devices my clients and their customers actually use. And the picture is consistent: iPhone is dominant among Ghanaian business owners and their upper-market customers.

This changes how I approach web design. Not "mobile-first" in the generic sense, but specifically Safari on iOS first. Here's what that means in practice.

Who your users actually are

Look at the analytics for any Ghanaian SMB website and you'll see the same story: 70–85% mobile traffic, with a significant portion from Safari. The business owner checking their own site is almost certainly on an iPhone. Their suppliers are on iPhones. Their upper-middle-class customers are on iPhones.

Android dominates volume in Ghana, especially on lower-cost devices. But for businesses targeting professionals, traders, and entrepreneurs — the people with purchasing power — Apple is the primary device.

What this means: If your site looks or works poorly on Safari iOS, you're failing your most important users. Chrome DevTools mobile emulation won't catch Safari-specific bugs. You need to test on a real iPhone.

Safari differences that actually bite you

Safari on iOS has quirks that Chrome-first developers get burned by:

  • 100vh includes the address bar. Use 100svh (small viewport height) instead to avoid content being hidden behind the browser UI.
  • Position: fixed behaves differently inside scroll containers. Test any sticky headers, modals, or bottom bars on a real device.
  • Input zoom. Safari zooms in when a text input has font-size below 16px. Set inputs to at least font-size: 16px to prevent this.
  • Backdrop-filter support requires the -webkit- prefix on older iOS versions.
  • Overscroll bounce. iOS has elastic scroll on body. If your app has a fixed layout, this can make it look broken. Use overscroll-behavior: none where needed.

The premium feel principle

There's a reason iPhone users pay a premium for their device — they care about quality and attention to detail. That same expectation carries over to the software they use. A site that looks beautiful and feels smooth on iOS signals quality. A site that has janky animations, misaligned text, or layout breaks on mobile signals the opposite — no matter how good the product or service is.

This is the Apple design principle applied to web: every interaction should feel intentional. Tap targets should be large enough. Transitions should be smooth. Text should be readable without zooming. Loading should be fast even on a cellular connection.

Practical checklist for iOS-first web

  • Use 100svh not 100vh for full-height sections
  • All form inputs: font-size: 16px minimum
  • Tap targets: 44×44px minimum (Apple's own HIG standard)
  • Test scroll performance — no jank on iPhone 11 or below
  • Add -webkit- prefixes for backdrop-filter, text-stroke
  • Check modals and overlays on real device for keyboard behaviour
  • Test with Safari's Responsive Design Mode and a real device

Connecting this to MarshalTech Hub

Every website I build through MarshalTech Hub is QA'd on an iPhone before it ships. Not because I want to be difficult — because that's the standard the client's customers expect. If a business owner is going to refer their site to a supplier who's checking it on an iPhone 14, it needs to be perfect on that device.

The businesses I build for are investing in their digital presence. They deserve software that matches the quality of the devices they use.
