From idea to live hotel site in 2 weeks

Abdul-Rashid Bushran · MarshalTech Hub · 7 min read

When the client for Nab Hotel & Events called me, the brief was straightforward: "We need a website. We have an event in two weeks and we want to be able to send people to it." No mockups, no existing brand guidelines, no content. Just a deadline.

This is how that project went — what I did, what went well, and what I'd change.

Week one: scope, structure, content

Day 1–2

Discovery and scope lock

One 90-minute call to understand the business. What rooms do they have? What events do they host? What do they want people to do on the site? I walked away with a clear list: Home, Rooms, Dining, Events, Gallery, Special Offers, Contact. That became the nav, and I didn't deviate from it.

Day 3

Content collection

I gave the client a simple WhatsApp checklist: logo file, room photos (at least 3 per room type), a short description for each room, and their contact details. No content, no site — I made this clear upfront. They delivered by end of day.

Day 4–5

HTML structure and base styles

I built the skeleton first — all pages, navigation, basic layout grid — before touching any visual design. This let me move content around easily and see what was missing.

Day 6–7

Design system and hero sections

Established the colour palette (deep green and gold — the hotel's existing colours), typography, and component patterns. Then built all hero sections while the system was fresh.

Week two: build, review, ship

Day 8–10

Interior pages and gallery

Rooms, dining, events pages. The gallery was the most time-consuming — I built a lightbox from scratch rather than using a library, which I'd reconsider next time.

Day 11

Booking and contact forms

Simple form-to-email setup using Formspree. The client wasn't ready for a full booking system, so I built an enquiry form that routes to their email and WhatsApp.

Day 12–13

Client review and revisions

Shared a preview link. Two rounds of revisions — mostly copy changes and one colour tweak. No structural changes. The upfront scope lock paid off here.

Day 14

Domain, hosting, launch

Connected the domain, pushed to production, ran final checks on mobile. Live by 6pm.

What went well

  • Locking scope on day one. Because we agreed exactly what pages existed before I wrote a line of code, there was no "can we add a blog?" on day 12.
  • Content-first approach. Waiting for real photos before building the gallery meant I built the layout around actual assets, not placeholders that never matched.
  • Building the design system before the pages. Having consistent spacing, colours, and components defined meant every page felt cohesive without me having to think about it.

What I'd do differently

  • Build the gallery with a library. I spent 4 hours on a lightbox that a well-maintained library would have given me in 20 minutes. Solo projects don't need custom everything.
  • Get the hosting decision on day one. The domain transfer took longer than expected and nearly pushed launch. Have the hosting conversation in the first call.
  • More mobile testing earlier. I caught a Safari layout issue on day 13. If I'd been testing on device from day 4, I'd have fixed it before it was urgent.
The real lesson: Two weeks is enough for a complete, professional website — if you spend the first 20% of that time making decisions rather than writing code. Scope clarity and content collection are not "admin tasks." They're the project.

The result

The site launched in time for the event. The client could share the URL with guests, and the contact form started receiving enquiries within the first week. Not a complex platform — but exactly what the business needed, delivered when they needed it.

That's the standard I hold myself to: not the most technically impressive solution, but the right solution, on time.
